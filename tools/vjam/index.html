Abstract

Decoupled video planning, faithful inverse dynamics

Video generative models have emerged as a promising robotics backbone, capable of generating videos that depict the completion of complex tasks across embodiments and environments. Many current approaches finetune video models with action-labeled data, turning them into robot foundation models that jointly predict future observations and actions.

We study an alternative, underexplored route: leave the video planner as is, and train an embodiment-specific inverse dynamics model (IDM). This decoupling lets the planner remain embodiment-agnostic, lets video backbones be swapped without re-training the IDM, and lets the IDM be trained from autonomous self-play data.

Our policy combines an action-free video world model with a structured IDM based on the robot embodiment Jacobian. The result is a faithful video-to-action translator that is data-efficient and scales to high-dimensional action spaces. We call this policy the Video–Jacobian–Action Model (VJAM); it achieves strong zero-shot performance on a real Panda arm and 16-DoF Allegro-hand cube re-orientation, and the same video planner drives both bodies via different embodiment-specific IDMs.

Method

A planner, a translator, a closed loop

The video model rolls out a short visual plan — a path along the image manifold. The Jacobian-IDM inverts each step into actions. The robot executes, re-observes, and the planner is re-queried. One primitive subsumes perception, planning, and control.

The Jacobian IDM

Generated video → Jacobian field → action

The Jacobian-IDM predicts a per-pixel tangent map from action perturbations to image-space motion. Given two consecutive predicted frames, it recovers actions by regularized least squares against this map — turning a generic video planner into an executable controller.

Why a structured IDM

Faithfulness, data-efficiency, scaling with DoFs

A controlled toy study isolates the contribution of structure. Constraining actions to enter through a learned tangent map — rather than regressing them from raw image pairs — pays off precisely where it matters: limited data, high-dimensional action spaces.

(a) Actions recovered from generated videos by direct-IDM baselines vs. J-IDM, for increasing degrees of freedom. (b) Under a fixed training budget, the direct-IDM gap to J-IDM widens as DoF grows. (c) In the high-DoF regime, J-IDM holds a better data–accuracy frontier.
